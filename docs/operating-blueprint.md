## Positioning

Aurion Labs is a modern operational digital systems company for local businesses. It builds and operates the digital infrastructure that helps gyms, clinics, salons, restaurants, coaching centers, and service businesses get leads, book customers, and run follow-up more reliably.

## What The Business Is Not

- Not a generic agency
- Not a freelance studio
- Not a template seller
- Not a hype AI startup

## Core Business Model

Aurion Labs should be sold as a productized service business with recurring infrastructure revenue.

Revenue should come from:

- Setup fees for design, build, and launch
- Monthly maintenance retainers
- Hosting and deployment fees
- WhatsApp and lead routing systems
- Booking and intake systems
- Dashboard and reporting subscriptions
- Automation and workflow support

## Current Model Audit

Current strengths:

- Premium frontend presentation
- Clearer WhatsApp conversion path
- Consultation-oriented contact funnel
- Services already framed around systems, not just pages
- Pricing already acknowledges infrastructure and support

Current weaknesses:

- Offer still reads partly project-based
- Recurring revenue is not yet dominant
- Backend value is not fully productized
- Support and maintenance are present but not deeply packaged
- Retention depends more on goodwill than system design
- SaaS potential exists, but the underlying modules are not yet standardized enough

## Scalability Weaknesses

- Too much scope can remain custom if offers are not tightly packaged
- Every new client can become a one-off unless the delivery engine is reused
- Manual onboarding slows growth
- Support work can consume founder time if tickets and SLAs are not structured
- No clear platform layer yet for multi-client operations

## Productization Opportunities

Package the work into repeatable modules:

- Website infrastructure
- Lead system
- Booking system
- WhatsApp routing system
- Lightweight operations dashboard
- Post-launch support layer
- Automation layer

Each module should have:

- A clear outcome
- A defined scope
- A standard setup process
- A recurring support option
- A path to expand later

## Recommended Offer Stack

### 1. Foundation

Best for businesses that need a stronger front door.

- Responsive website
- Core service pages
- Contact and WhatsApp routing
- Basic SEO
- Deployment setup
- Monitoring and maintenance option

### 2. Operational

Best for businesses that need conversion and intake.

- Website foundation
- Booking flow
- Lead capture
- WhatsApp handoff
- Forms and routing logic
- Monthly support
- Content updates

### 3. Infrastructure

Best for businesses that need recurring operational support.

- Website + booking system
- Dashboard and lead visibility
- Maintenance and uptime monitoring
- Workflow automation
- Support tickets
- Reporting and optimization

### 4. Growth System

Best for multi-location or more complex businesses.

- Multi-step workflows
- Custom backend modules
- Admin control center
- Analytics and conversion tracking
- Recurring optimization
- Integration support

## Pricing Architecture

Use a setup fee plus recurring fee model.

Example structure:

- Setup fee: design, build, launch
- Monthly base fee: hosting, monitoring, small edits
- Monthly managed fee: support, updates, workflow care
- Monthly infrastructure fee: booking, dashboards, automation, reporting

The goal is to move every client toward an ongoing subscription.

## Recurring Revenue Stack

Recommended recurring lines:

- Hosting subscription
- Maintenance subscription
- Support subscription
- Booking system subscription
- Lead routing subscription
- Dashboard subscription
- Automation subscription

Retention improves when the client depends on the system every week, not only when they need a redesign.

## Operational Practices

### Operational Principles

- Execution over perfection
- Reuse before rebuilding
- Operational clarity over visual complexity
- Systems before scale
- Standardize before automating
- Recurring value over one-time projects
- Sustainable operations over rapid chaos
- Long-term maintainability over short-term hacks

### Client Qualification & Rejection

Aurion Labs should selectively accept clients. Reject or avoid projects where:

- Payment reliability appears weak
- Scope is undefined
- Timelines are unrealistic
- Communication is disorganized
- Expectations are excessive
- Unlimited revisions are expected
- The client constantly changes direction
- The project has high operational chaos

High-risk indicators:

- “Small changes only” repeated excessively
- Vague requirements
- Delayed responses during onboarding
- Refusal to use structured workflows
- Comparing every task to cheap freelancers
- Expecting instant replies at all hours
- Requesting enterprise-level systems on low budgets

This protects founder bandwidth and operational stability.

### Support Structure & SLA

Support should be packaged and operationally limited.

#### Basic Support

- 48–72h response window
- Minor fixes only
- No structural changes

#### Managed Support

- 24–48h response
- Content edits
- Monitoring
- Small workflow improvements

#### Infrastructure Support

- Priority queue
- Workflow troubleshooting
- Operational assistance
- Dashboard and automation support

Emergency support should be limited and separately defined. Also define working hours, communication channels, emergency definition, and revision caps.

### Scope Protection Rules

To prevent operational chaos:

- Approved designs are considered locked
- Major workflow changes require separate quotes
- Infrastructure migrations are separate projects
- Additional integrations may affect timeline and pricing
- Repeated direction changes may pause the project
- Delayed client feedback affects delivery timelines

This prevents scope creep collapse.

### Founder Capacity Management

Operational scaling must respect founder bandwidth. Early-stage limits should exist for active projects, support clients, onboarding volume, weekly meetings, and custom backend requests.

When operational load exceeds safe limits:

- Onboarding pauses temporarily
- Lower-value custom work is reduced
- Reusable systems take priority

This prevents burnout-driven operational degradation.

### Operational Economics

Every project creates support burden, infrastructure cost, deployment responsibility, maintenance overhead, and communication overhead.

Low-ticket custom projects can become operationally expensive if revisions are unlimited, support boundaries are weak, or workflows are not standardized.

Recurring infrastructure revenue is required to sustain hosting, monitoring, maintenance, support operations, and future system development.

### Communication Rules

Aurion Labs should operate with structured communication. Recommended rules:

- Primary communication channel defined during onboarding
- Async-first communication preferred
- Support requests routed through structured forms/tickets
- Meetings used only for major milestones
- Revision requests grouped together
- Emergency escalation rules clearly defined

This reduces operational interruption chaos.

### Standard Delivery Checklist

Every project should pass a standard delivery process:

- Mobile responsiveness tested
- Forms tested
- WhatsApp flow tested
- CTA flow validated
- Performance reviewed
- SEO basics configured
- Backups enabled
- Analytics connected
- Deployment verified
- Recovery path documented

This creates consistent delivery quality.

### Failure & Incident Response

Operational systems can fail. Aurion Labs should maintain deployment rollback capability, backup systems, infrastructure monitoring, outage communication process, and recovery documentation.

Third-party providers may experience outages, API failures, DNS issues, or hosting instability. Operational resilience is more important than perfect uptime promises.

### Client Ownership Boundaries

Ownership responsibilities should remain explicit. Clarify who owns domains, who controls hosting, who manages deployments, source-code access policies, and infrastructure responsibility boundaries.

Reusable internal systems remain Aurion Labs property unless explicitly transferred.

### Offboarding Procedure

If a client exits:

- Credentials are transferred
- Backups may be exported
- Hosting responsibilities clarified
- Support access removed
- Infrastructure dependencies documented
- Maintenance obligations terminated

A structured offboarding process reduces operational disputes.

### Premature SaaS Warning

SaaS should not be forced prematurely. Risks include high support burden, excessive customization, unstable workflows, weak repetition, and operational complexity.

Reusable internal systems should mature through repeated real-world usage before becoming standalone SaaS products.

### Internal Tooling Roadmap

Aurion Labs should gradually build internal operational tooling such as a deployment manager, client tracker, renewal tracker, support center, screenshot automation, template registry, monitoring dashboard, and operational reporting tools. Internal operational efficiency compounds over time.
